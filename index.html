The Ladder of Causation

Pearl's key insight: correlation ≠ causation is not just a warning — it's a formal distinction. Three distinct questions require three different mathematical tools.

RUNG 1 · ASSOCIATION
P(Y | X=x) — seeing, observing. "What does observing X tell me about Y?"
RUNG 2 · INTERVENTION
P(Y | do(X=x)) — doing. "What happens to Y if I set X to x?"
RUNG 3 · COUNTERFACTUAL
P(Yₓ=y' | X=x, Y=y) — imagining. "Would Y have been different had X been x'?"

Backdoor Criterion

A set S blocks all backdoor paths from X to Y (paths with arrow pointing into X). Once blocked, the naive regression equals the causal effect.

P(Y|do(X)) = Σ_s P(Y|X,S=s)·P(S=s)

Direct vs Indirect Effects

X → Y (direct) and X → M → Y (via mediator M). Pearl's mediation formula decomposes total effect into direct + indirect.

TE = DE + IE (linear case)
IE = Σ_m E[Y|x,m]·[P(m|x')−P(m|x)]

Example: Gender → Qualification → Hiring, plus Gender → Hiring directly (discrimination).

Probability of Necessity (PN)

Given a patient took the drug and recovered, what's the probability they would have died without it? This is PN — a Rung 3 (counterfactual) query.

PN = P(Yx'=0 | X=x, Y=1)

With monotonicity assumption (drug can only help), Pearl shows:

PN ≥ (RR − 1)/RR where RR = P(Y=1|X=1)/P(Y=1|X=0)
